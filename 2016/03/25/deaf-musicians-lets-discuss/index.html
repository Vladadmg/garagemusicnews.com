Deaf musicians, let’s discuss

Posted on March 25, 2016 by THE GARAGE in Essay // 0 Comments

by Ryan Johnson

“Deaf” and “music” are two words that are rarely seen in the same sentence. “Deaf musician” is something seen even less often. Of course, we all know the story of Beethoven going Deaf and continuing composing, but did you know some of his music may be as memorable as it is because he was Deaf? The way he felt the vibrations of his piano was different than how he heard them, leading him to compose very innovative and everlasting music.

But this is totally a rarity in our world today, right? Not quite. It’s not that Deaf musicians aren’t there, it’s that few look for them. It isn’t really shocking that Deaf musicians are an underappreciated bunch within the normal establishment of music, which is structured for a hearing world. As an American Sign Language (ASL) student, I was elated to discover that my love for both ASL and music has a crossover. While there is a variety of rock bands, percussionists, and singers that are Deaf, my favorite artists to watch and listen to are Deaf rappers.

Signmark (Marko Vuoriheimo) is a Finnish rapper who creates his raps in International Sign Language, which is a sort of summary of various national sign languages that creates a way for the international communication for the Deaf. He chooses to not use his spoken voice, so his albums feature rapper Brandon Bauer who vocally performs his rapping. Signmark’s albums come out as CD/DVD combos, to allow easy access to his music for the hearing as well as the Deaf. Like many rappers, his songs feature a variety of life topics, but “Our Life (Deaf Rap)” focuses on discrimination he has faced being a Deaf individual, as well as touching on Deaf history.

Signmark has a unique perspective and his music carries a message that most will never learn. Many of his songs, like my favorite “Smells Like Victory,” don’t let on he is Deaf, but Deafness is a message in majority of the body of his work. Since being a Deaf musician is somewhat of a rarity these artists feature their Deaf experience quite strongly in their work, to bring awareness and education. So it was significant when Signmark became the first Deaf recording artist to become signed by an international recording label, Warner Music. That event wasn’t only exciting for Vuoriheimo, but was a milestone for Deaf musicians, as well as the Deaf community. There are various testaments to his impact on the Deaf community, from grateful students inspired by his success to politicians who say his music is changing the way the world interprets Deafness.

Signmark fortunately isn’t the only Deaf rapper on my playlists. Sean Forbes is a Deaf rapper a little closer to home, based out of Michigan. Forbes raps in ASL, but performs his pieces verbally as well. His 2012 album Perfect Imperfection is a full LP of his raps, which have an interesting turn. The Deaf perspective is highlighted on the album in many songs in a variety of ways. A favorite off of Perfect Imperfection is “Def Deaf Girls,” which flirts with the stereotype of rap songs about girls at clubs, but instead highlights the Deaf community’s women. Another, “Let’s Mambo,” mocks the stereotype of Deaf individuals not being able to dance. The video especially proves that idea wrong.

These tracks all have a very unique window into the Deaf community that a lot of hearing individuals would never have thought of, placed in a comfort space for “hearies” music.

Though they’re a small group, Deaf rappers create great songs while making the world of music more accessible to the Deaf community. Sean Forbes, while not rapping, works on the company he co-founded, The Deaf Professional Arts Network (D-PAN). D-PAN is a media platform to promote Deaf artists in a variety of mediums, while also creating and producing music videos of popular songs into ASL.The accessibility of music, as well as the beauty and fluidity of ASL is something that hearing musicians are also beginning to catch on to. Earlier this month American Authors released an official video for their radio mix of popular song “Pride” with Deaf broadway actress Sandra Mae Frank doing an ASL interpretation. While the song itself is lovely, the beauty of Frank’s signing and expression is what has lead to this being on repeat for the last few weeks.

As most music fans know, music is more than just sound. Music is a way to provide feeling, to create a story and call forth different human connections. Why should the Deaf community be excluded from that because they simply can’t listen with their ears? There is so much more to it than just listening. Deaf musicians provide a very unique and valuable perspective in a space that we have made it hard for them to succeed, but succeeded they have.
